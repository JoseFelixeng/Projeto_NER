This work presents an iterative deep learning framework for rst-break detection in seismic
data processing that prioritizes dataset-specic adaptability over universal generalization.
The methodology combines a U-Net architecture with domain-specic quality control
mechanisms to expand the training dataset from limited initial annotations. Experiments
conducted on two Canadian mining seismic datasets demonstrate the framework’s eec-
tiveness, with the Halfmile dataset showing improvements through iterative training -
reducing mean absolute error from 16.16 to 9.16 samples while maintaining high window
accuracy (0.9677). Results from the Sudbury dataset reveal the framework’s sensibility
to data quality and underlying annotation consistency. These ndings suggest potential
benets in developing workows that adapt to specic survey characteristics.
Keywords: deep learning; seismic processing; rst breaks; iterative training; quality
control.

The accurate detection of seismic rst breaks is a fundamental task in geophysical
exploration and data processing. First breaks, which correspond to the initial arrivals of
seismic energy at receiver locations, are critical for a variety of preprocessing steps in
seismic workows. These include static corrections, which account for near-surface velocity
variations and topographic eects, as well as applications such as velocity modeling
and travel time tomography (Marsden, 1993). The accurate identication of these rst
breaks signicantly inuences the quality of subsequent seismic imaging and interpretation
processes.
Traditional approaches to rst-break picking, such as the Short-Term Average/Long-
Term Average (STA/LTA) (Coppens, 1985) method and the Modied Energy Ratio (MER)
method (Wong, 2016), have demonstrated eectiveness in datasets with high signal-to-
noise ratios (SNR). However, these methods face considerable limitations in scenarios
with signicant noise contamination, weak signals, or complex acquisition geometries.
Moreover, the manual renement of picks in such challenging conditions is an extremely
time-consuming process, often requiring days or even months for large-scale seismic datasets.
This underscores the limitations of traditional algorithms and highlights the importance of
developing automated, robust, and scalable solutions that can signicantly reduce human
eort and expedite seismic data processing workows.
Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly in the domain of deep learn-
ing (see Goodfellow et al. (2016)), have provided new tools for addressing these challenges.
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (O’Shea and Nash, 2015), with their ability to
learn spatial hierarchies and capture intricate patterns, have become a cornerstone for
various tasks in seismic data analysis. Among these, the U-Net architecture (Ronneberger
et al., 2015) has emerged as a particularly eective solution due to its encoder-decoder
structure with skip connections. Originally designed for biomedical image segmentation, the
U-Net’s ability to integrate ne-grained spatial details with broader contextual information
has proven highly valuable in the seismic domain. Nevertheless, while these methods have
introduced substantial improvements, issues such as generalization across datasets, noise
robustness, and the need for large volumes of labeled data remain key challenges.
To address these challenges, this work presents an iterative framework for rst-
break detection based on a U-Net architecture. This framework is designed to operate
eectively with limited annotated data, leveraging iterative self-training and domain-specic
of automated rst-break picking.

2.1
Machine Learning and Deep Neural Networks
Computer science emerged from the systematic study of computation and algo-
rithmic abstraction—the practice of decomposing problems into discrete, well-dened
procedures. This foundation rests on the premise that problems can be solved through de-
terministic sequences of operations. However, a signicant class of computational challenges,
including pattern recognition and natural language understanding, resist traditional algo-
rithmic decomposition due to their inherent complexity and variability. These challenges
motivated the development of statistical learning approaches.
The central problem in supervised learning is fundamentally one of function approx-
imation: given a set of input-output pairs (x1, y1), ▷▷▷, (xn, yn), the objective is to construct
a mapping f : X →Y that both ts the observed data and generalizes eectively to new
instances. This framing transforms the challenge from explicit programming to learning
from examples, where the goal is to discover underlying patterns rather than specify them
directly.
The theoretical and practical pursuit of this question has produced a rich framework
of methods,from support vector machines to deep neural networks, that now serve as
fundamental tools across disciplines. These approaches have proven particularly powerful for
problems characterized by high dimensionality, noise, and complex non-linear relationships,
enabling advances in elds ranging from computer vision to natural language processing.
Neural networks emerged as a particularly powerful solution framework for these
pattern recognition challenges. The architecture consists of hierarchical layers of compu-
tational units connected through learnable parameters W(l) at each layer l. Each layer
through gradient descent on a loss function L, dened as the discrepancy between network
predictions and target outputs: minW,b L(ˆy, y) (Goodfellow et al., 2016).
The expressive power of deep architectures through hierarchical representations
introduces fundamental challenges in model design. While increased model complexity
enables the capture of more intricate patterns, it imposes greater computational demands
and heightens the risk of overtting—where the model learns spurious correlations specic
to the training data rather than generalizable relationships. This tension between model
complexity and generalization capability, formally characterized as the bias-variance trade-
o, motivated the development of specialized architectures optimized for specic data
modalities.

relies on analyzing elastic or acoustic waves that propagate through the Earth: when these

Data acquisition geometries are optimized according to survey objectives and

multiple source points, while marine surveys utilize vessel-towed hydrophone streamers.

The recorded data is organized into gathers based on common acquisition parameters,

by various noise sources: near-surface eects, ambient noise, and multiple reections all
obscure the primary reections of interest (Biloti, 2024; Yilmaz, 2001). Modern surveys
generate massive datasets as acquisitions routinely produce hundreds of thousands of
traces. Thus, the scale and complexity of seismic data, combined with the presence of
various noise types, demands for extensive pre-processing and processing steps to obtain
accurate subsurface images.
2.3
First Break Picking
First breaks in seismic data represent the initial arrival times of seismic energy at
receiver locations. Their accurate identication is crucial for seismic data pre-processing,
particularly in land acquisitions where they enable static corrections—time shifts applied
to each trace to compensate for near-surface velocity variations and topographic eects
2.3.1
Traditional Methods
The increasing scale of seismic surveys has motivated the development of automated
rst-break picking methods. Early approaches focused on detecting abrupt changes in
signal characteristics. The Short-Term Average/Long-Term Average (STA/LTA) method
(Coppens, 1985) compares energy ratios between short and long time windows to identify
sudden amplitude variations. While these methods perform adequately on data with high
signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), their reliability deteriorates in noisy conditions due to their
susceptibility to spurious amplitude spikes.
Further developments sought to enhance robustness to noise. The Modied Energy
Ratio (MER) method (Wong, 2016) incorporates preprocessing techniques such as multi-
trace summation to improve SNR before applying the picking algorithm. While MER
shows improved performance under moderate noise conditions, it remains susceptible
to errors when processing weak signals or data with severe noise contamination. An
alternative approach based on fractal analysis (Boschetti et al., 1996) identies rst breaks
by detecting variations in fractal dimension along seismic traces, attempting to capture
the transition from noise to signal. However, these methods, despite their sophistication in
emulating human pattern recognition, impose signicant computational costs while still
exhibiting limitations in low SNR scenarios
Despite these advancements, a signicant limitation shared by many of these al-
gorithms is their reliance on processing traces individually, which overlooks the spatial
coherence between adjacent traces and exacerbates inaccuracies under challenging con-
ditions. Consequently, automated picking methods often struggle to achieve satisfactory
results when dealing with low-quality or noisy data, requiring substantial manual interven-
tion to correct errors and rene picks. As a result, a considerable amount of human eort
is still necessary to ensure reliable rst-break picking in complex datasets.

tern recognition capabilities of modern machine learning techniques prove particularly
advantageous, as demonstrated by numerous studies evaluating their eectiveness. The
problem is commonly formulated as a semantic segmentation task, where each seismogram
is processed as an image whose pixels are classied into predened classes.

Loginov et al. (2022) introduced a 1D convolutional network workow that achieves
comparable results to U-Net while requiring fewer computational resources and only
thousands of traces for training. However, the applicability of their model across dierent
seismic settings remains uncertain, as 1D CNNs cannot capture the spatial context that is
crucial in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) scenarios, where individual traces may become
indistinguishable from noise without considering their neighboring traces.
Both Li et al. (2024) and Yuan et al. (2020) propose more sophisticated workows. Li
et al. (2024) employs a parallel structure combining a U-Net and a denoising convolutional
network to implement a ne-tuning approach that incorporates knowledge of noisy data.
Yuan et al. (2020) introduces a dual-model architecture comprising a U-Net for semantic
segmentation and a recurrent neural network for selecting rst picks among the inferred
classes. The authors develop a straightforward transfer learning scheme where weights from
a previously trained network are applied when training on dierent eld data geometries,
using only a few newly annotated shots. Their results demonstrate that this approach
yields improved evaluation metrics compared to naive weight initialization.
Similarly, Mardan et al. (2024) explored transfer learning by ne-tuning pre-trained
ImageNet networks with minimal new annotated examples. However, while their results
suggested improved performance through pre-training, the validation was limited by both
a small test set and ambiguous evaluation metrics.
These approaches aim to leverage knowledge gained from networks trained on
initial datasets through weight ne-tuning, thus minimizing the need for new manual
annotations. This strategy for generalization diers fundamentally from St-Charles et al.
(2023)’s approach of learning generalizable features from one dataset for direct application
to others. However, both methodologies require substantial previously annotated gathers
for initial training. For such approaches to be eciently employed, their generalization
capacity must be robust enough to minimize further intensive data preparation needs.
Proper validation of these methods would require testing on large volumes of fully annotated
gathers—a challenge given the typical access restrictions on seismic data. Nevertheless,
St-Charles et al. (2023) advances this eort by releasing labeled data for four distinct eld
datasets.

Pu et al. (2024) developed a three-step validation process: statistical ltering, trace

Aligning with Loginov et al. (2022), we argue that rst break picking models
should not prioritize high generalizability. Although CNNs exhibit translation and rotation
invariance, they lack scale invariance. The model lters learn features specic to their
training seismograms’ resolution and therefore struggle to generalize across geometries
with varying trace counts per gather and sampling rates. While theoretically possible to
learn scale-invariant features by training on diverse datasets with dierent resolutions,
this would require more complex architectures capable of learning a broader feature set,
substantially increasing computational demands. Moreover, even such enhanced models
could fail when encountering signicantly dierent resolutions. This practical limitation is
supported by Zwartjes and Yoo (2022), who found that network performance improves as
input sizing approaches original dimensions. Furthermore, seismic images lack the rich,
distinctive details that enable convolutional models to capture universal features, as they
do with natural images
Therefore, we propose that a reliable automated workow for rst break picking
should emphasize a precise framework capable of generalizing picks from limited examples,
while incorporating robust validation mechanisms to ensure reliability. Such an approach
would aim to reduce manual labeling requirements through eective use of limited training
data and systematic quality control of predictions.

3.1
Framework Overview
The proposed iterative training framework consists of several interconnected stages,
as illustrated in Figure 2. The workow begins with dataset and model initialization,
followed by the selection of initial training gathers. These gathers are split into training
and validation sets to provide the foundation for the rst training cycle.
The core of the framework is an iterative loop that progressively expands the labeled
dataset. Each iteration consists of three main phases: model training, inference on new
gathers, and quality control. After training, the model selects new gathers for inference
based on their spatial distribution and signal quality. These predictions then undergo a
two-stage quality control process.
The quality control process begins with a condence ltering step, where predictions
are evaluated based on their softmax probabilities. Subsequently, a tau-oset analysis
ensures spatial coherence of the picks within each gather. Gathers that pass both quality
control stages have their picks incorporated into the training dataset, while failed gathers
are discarded from the current iteration.
After each iteration, the framework checks dataset coverage to determine if the
desired proportion of total gathers (50% in our experiments) has been used in training,
validation, or inference. If this threshold has not been reached, the process continues with
updated training and validation sets incorporating the newly validated picks.
The nal evaluation phase assesses model performance specically on gathers that
were not part of the initial labeled set. This provides an assessment of how well the model
learned to replicate human picking behavior using both the original manual picks and the
iteratively added predictions.

The implementation uses a U-Net architecture for seismic data segmentation. The
network processes seismic gathers as input, with dimensions of traces × time samples,
applying padding to accommodate varying gather sizes. The architecture follows an
encoder-decoder structure with skip connections.
While various deep learning architectures could potentially be employed for this
task, we focus on the U-Net architecture to demonstrate our iterative training framework.
This choice is motivated by U-Net’s proven eectiveness in seismic interpretation tasks, as
demonstrated by previous studies (St-Charles et al., 2023; Zwartjes and Yoo, 2022).
Each convolution block in the network consists of two sequential 3×3 convolutions.
The convolutions use zero padding with size 1 and stride 1, maintaining spatial dimensions
while expanding the feature space. These convolutions operate without bias terms, relying
on the subsequent batch normalization layers to center the feature distributions.
Following each convolution, a batch normalization layer standardizes the feature
maps, facilitating stable gradient ow during training. The normalized outputs pass
through a leaky ReLU activation function with a negative slope of 0.01, allowing for small
negative gradients that help prevent dead neurons.
The contracting path applies these double convolution blocks sequentially, with each
block followed by a 2×2 max pooling operation. This progression reduces spatial dimensions
while increasing feature depth through [64, 128, 256, 512] channels. The expanding path
mirrors this structure, using transposed convolutions for upsampling. Skip connections
transfer feature maps from the contracting path to the upsampled outputs, maintaining
spatial precision through the decoding process. The nal layer applies a 1×1 convolution
to produce two feature maps matching the input dimensions, representing the background
and rst-break classes.

Previous studies have implemented various data augmentation methods, including

Amplitude normalization forms a key component of the preprocessing pipeline.
The process scales samples to values between zero and 65,535, mapping to the unsigned
16-bit integer range. This quantization serves two purposes: it reduces noise in the signal
representation and limits amplitude variation precision. This approach draws from computer
vision applications, where three 8-bit channels provide sucient precision for complex
feature extraction tasks.
To address the eects of geometric spreading in seismic data, where wave amplitude
decreases with the square of traveled distance, normalization occurs on a per-trace basis.
This method eectively implements a gain control, enhancing later arrivals and far-oset
signals while maintaining relative amplitude relationships within individual traces.
importance. The focus here was on establishing a reliable baseline preprocessing pipeline
that enables eective model training while maintaining computational eciency.
3.5
Iterative Training Workﬂow
The workow implements a self-training approach to expand the training dataset
while maintaining a quality control. Beginning with a small set of manually labeled data,
the process iteratively incorporates new predictions that meet specic condence and
coherency criteria.
3.5.1
Training Process
The training uses the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and batch size
of 4, beginning with 5% of available gathers. Each iteration processes 200 new gathers
for potential inclusion in the training set, maintaining a 75% training and 25% validation
split. To prevent overtting while preserving learned features, the weights reset every 5
iterations, with each training cycle comprising 30 epochs. This balance between weight
preservation and reset helps maintain model stability while allowing adaptation to new
data.
This iterative approach inherently provides some protection against error prop-
agation. Samples included in earlier iterations participate in more training cycles than
those added later, giving the original manually-picked examples greater inuence over the
nal model. This weighted inuence helps maintain model stability despite the potential
accumulation of prediction errors.
3.5.2

The quality control system implements two sequential ltering steps to ensure
reliable prediction incorporation:
1. Condence Filtering: The model outputs undergo softmax transformation to ob-
tain prediction probabilities. The process retains only picks with condence values
exceeding 0.70, ensuring high prediction certainty.
2. Spatial Coherence Analysis: Following the method described in Loginov et al. (2022),
the process evaluates predictions through tau-oset crossplots. First-arrival predic-

The iterative process continues until reaching 15 iterations, ensuring completion of
a full 5-iteration training cycle after the nal reset. Model evaluation uses only gathers ex-
cluded from the initial training set, providing an assessment of the self-training procedure’s
eectiveness in expanding the labeled dataset.
The threshold values chosen for both ltering steps (0.70 for condence and 2σ for
spatial coherence) were selected based on initial experimentation. While these parameters
could be optimized through systematic testing, they provided a reasonable balance between
prediction reliability and data retention in our experiments.
The quality control process inherently introduces a selection bias towards picks that
conform to expected patterns. While this improves overall reliability, it may exclude valid
but unusual picks, particularly in geologically complex areas. For example, in areas with
strong lateral velocity variations, valid picks might be rejected by the spatial coherence
criteria despite being correct.

4.1
First-Break Window Region
The introduction of a window region around rst breaks aims to account for
uncertainty inherent in manual picking and provide a more stable training target. Tables 4
and 3 present the results comparing models trained with and without a rst-break window
region. The experiments used a xed data split of 3.75% for training, 1.25% for validation,
and 95% for testing across the datasets.
The window-based approach shows lower exact accuracy (Aexact) compared to
the single-line approach, as the model must identify a specic sample within the target
window. The window-based approach achieves lower mean absolute error (MAE) across
both datasets: Halfmile (16.16 vs 29.79 samples) and Sudbury (22.69 vs 25.05 samples).
The window-based method demonstrates more stable performance, with lower
standard deviations in both MAE and window accuracy (Awindow). For the Sudbury
dataset, the standard deviation of Awindow decreases from 0.1262 to 0.0367. The standard
deviations of the metrics provide insight into method reliability. The window-based
approach shows consistent standard deviations across datasets for Aexact (0.0172-0.0255),
while the single-line approach shows higher variation, particularly for Sudbury (0.1486).
The impact of the window-based approach varies across datasets. Halfmile shows
the highest window accuracy (0.9375) while maintaining the lowest MAE (16.16 samples),
suggesting that its rst breaks may be more consistently dened. In contrast, Sudbury
exhibits lower window accuracy (0.8136) despite having moderate MAE values, indicating
that are more consistently within the acceptable range of true rst breaks. This aligns with
seismic processing requirements, where small variations in pick location have limited impact
on subsequent processing steps. The lower MAE values in the window-based approach
suggest better overall pick positioning, even when exact matches are not achieved.

This work presented an iterative deep learning framework for rst-break detec-
tion that prioritizes adaptability to specic seismic datasets over pursuit of universal
generalization. The Halfmile dataset showed signicant improvements through iterative

5.1
Limitations
While these results demonstrate the potential of our adaptable workow approach,
some limitations should be acknowledged that represent opportunities for future research
and potential improvements to the methodology.
The number of experimental runs (10 repetitions per conguration) limits the
statistical robustness of our results. A larger number of trials would provide more reliable
estimates of the model’s performance variability and enable more rigorous statistical
analysis. This limitation was primarily due to computational resource and time constraints.
Our quality control approach relies on empirically chosen parameters without
extensive optimization. The potential biases introduced by the condence thresholding
and spatial coherence ltering were not thoroughly investigated. Alternative QC metrics
or approaches might provide better selection criteria for the iterative training process.
Furthermore, the interaction between QC parameters and nal model performance remains
to be systematically analyzed.
Some aspects of the training process warrant further investigation. The impact of
weight reset intervals on model convergence and stability was not extensively studied. The
chosen training/validation split ratios and learning rate policies were based on common
practices rather than systematic optimization. Alternative training schedules might provide
model performance remains unexplored.
5.2
Future Work
Future research should extend this philosophy of adaptable workows while sys-
tematically addressing current limitations. Key areas for investigation include:
1. Comprehensive ablation studies to:
• Quantify the impact of preprocessing choices
• Evaluate the necessity of weight reset intervals
• Assess the sensitivity to training hyperparameters
2. Renement of quality control mechanisms through:
• Systematic optimization of condence and spatial coherence thresholds
• Development of metrics incorporating geophysical domain knowledge
• Investigation of alternative validation approaches
3. Improvement of training data quality by:
• Establishing rigorous criteria for manual pick selection
• Developing methods to assess ground truth reliability
• Creating validation protocols for expert annotations
4. Enhancement of the training process via:
• Optimization of weight reset strategies
• Investigation of adaptive learning rate schedules
• Development of dynamic training/validation split criteria
These directions for future research underscore a key insight from our work: the path
to better automated rst-break picking may not lie in pursuing universal generalization, but
by our experimental results, suggests a practical way forward for reducing manual eort in
seismic processing while maintaining high standards for pick quality. As seismic datasets
continue to grow in size and complexity, such adaptable workows will become increasingly
valuable for maintaining eciency without sacricing reliability.
