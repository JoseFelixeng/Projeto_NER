ABSTRACT This work presents an iterative deep learning framework for rst-break detection in seismic data processing that prioritizes dataset-specic adaptability over universal generalization. The methodology combines a U-Net architecture with domain-specic quality control mechanisms to expand the training dataset from limited initial annotations. Experiments conducted on two Canadian mining seismic datasets demonstrate the framework’s eec- tiveness, with the Halfmile dataset showing improvements through iterative training - reducing mean absolute error from .to .samples while maintaining high window accuracy (.). Results from the Sudbury dataset reveal the framework’s sensibility to data quality and underlying annotation consistency. These ndings suggest potential benets in developing workows that adapt to specic survey characteristics. Keywords: deep learning; seismic processing; rst breaks; iterative training; quality control. INTRODUCTION The accurate detection of seismic rst breaks is a fundamental task in geophysical exploration and data processing. First breaks, which correspond to the initial arrivals of seismic energy at receiver locations, are critical for a variety of preprocessing steps in seismic workows. These include static corrections, which account for near-surface velocity variations and topographic eects, as well as applications such as velocity modeling and travel time tomography (Marsden, ). The accurate identication of these rst breaks signicantly inuences the quality of subsequent seismic imaging and interpretation processes. Traditional approaches to rst-break picking, such as the Short-Term Average/Long- Term Average (STA/LTA) (Coppens, ) method and the Modied Energy Ratio (MER) method (Wong, ), have demonstrated eectiveness in datasets with high signal-to- noise ratios (SNR). However, these methods face considerable limitations in scenarios with signicant noise contamination, weak signals, or complex acquisition geometries. Moreover, the manual renement of picks in such challenging conditions is an extremely time-consuming process, often requiring days or even months for large-scale seismic datasets. This underscores the limitations of traditional algorithms and highlights the importance of developing automated, robust, and scalable solutions that can signicantly reduce human eort and expedite seismic data processing workows. Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly in the domain of deep learn- ing (see Goodfellow et al. ()), have provided new tools for addressing these challenges. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (O’Shea and Nash, ), with their ability to learn spatial hierarchies and capture intricate patterns, have become a cornerstone for various tasks in seismic data analysis. Among these, the U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., ) has emerged as a particularly eective solution due to its encoder-decoder structure with skip connections. Originally designed for biomedical image segmentation, the U-Net’s ability to integrate ne-grained spatial details with broader contextual information has proven highly valuable in the seismic domain. Nevertheless, while these methods have introduced substantial improvements, issues such as generalization across datasets, noise robustness, and the need for large volumes of labeled data remain key challenges. To address these challenges, this work presents an iterative framework for rst- break detection based on a U-Net architecture. This framework is designed to operate eectively with limited annotated data, leveraging iterative self-training and domain-specic quality control mechanisms to expand the training dataset progressively. By incorporating preprocessing strategies tailored to seismic data characteristics and systematic quality Chapter . It also presents the motivation for this study and the proposed solution. • Chapter reviews the theoretical foundations and existing literature relevant to rst-break picking. It details the U-Net architecture employed, the preprocessing steps tailored for seismic data, and the iterative training workow. Furthermore, the chapter outlines the experimental setup and the evaluation metrics used to assess the performance of the proposed framework. • Chapter presents the results obtained through the proposed framework, along with a discussion of their implications. The chapter evaluates the framework’s eectiveness in improving rst-break detection and highlights its strengths and limitations. • Chapter concludes the monograph by summarizing the key ndings, discussing the broader implications of this research, and suggesting directions for future work. Through this structure, this work aims to provide a comprehensive exploration of the proposed framework for rst-break detection, situating it within the broader context of seismic data processing and machine learning. LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETI- CAL FOUNDATION This chapter presents the theoretical foundation and literature review necessary to understand and contextualize our work. We begin by introducing neural networks and their evolution toward specialized architectures for spatial data analysis. We then provide an overview of seismic data acquisition and processing, with particular focus on the challenges that motivate our research. Finally, we review the development of rst break picking methods, from traditional approaches to current deep learning solutions, identifying limitations and opportunities that inform our methodology. .Machine Learning and Deep Neural Networks Computer science emerged from the systematic study of computation and algo- rithmic abstraction—the practice of decomposing problems into discrete, well-dened procedures. This foundation rests on the premise that problems can be solved through de- terministic sequences of operations. However, a signicant class of computational challenges, including pattern recognition and natural language understanding, resist traditional algo- rithmic decomposition due to their inherent complexity and variability. These challenges motivated the development of statistical learning approaches. The central problem in supervised learning is fundamentally one of function approx- imation: given a set of input-output pairs (x, y), ▷▷▷, (xn, yn), the objective is to construct a mapping f : X →Y that both ts the observed data and generalizes eectively to new instances. This framing transforms the challenge from explicit programming to learning from examples, where the goal is to discover underlying patterns rather than specify them directly. The theoretical and practical pursuit of this question has produced a rich framework of methods,from support vector machines to deep neural networks, that now serve as fundamental tools across disciplines. These approaches have proven particularly powerful for problems characterized by high dimensionality, noise, and complex non-linear relationships, enabling advances in elds ranging from computer vision to natural language processing. Neural networks emerged as a particularly powerful solution framework for these pattern recognition challenges. The architecture consists of hierarchical layers of compu- tational units connected through learnable parameters W(l) at each layer l. Each layer implements a transformation of the form h(l+) = σ(W(l)h(l) + b(l)), where σ is a nonlin- Chapter . Literature Review and Theoretical Foundation ear activation function. This sequential composition of linear and nonlinear operations progressively projects the input data into increasingly abstract feature spaces, enabling the network to discover complex hierarchical patterns. The parameters are optimized through gradient descent on a loss function L, dened as the discrepancy between network predictions and target outputs: minW,b L(ˆy, y) (Goodfellow et al., ). The expressive power of deep architectures through hierarchical representations introduces fundamental challenges in model design. While increased model complexity enables the capture of more intricate patterns, it imposes greater computational demands and heightens the risk of overtting—where the model learns spurious correlations specic to the training data rather than generalizable relationships. This tension between model complexity and generalization capability, formally characterized as the bias-variance trade- o, motivated the development of specialized architectures optimized for specic data modalities. A signicant architectural innovation emerged in the form of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), which exploit spatial structure through local connectivity patterns and parameter sharing. Formally, each convolutional layer implements the operation h(l+) i,j = σ(  m,n W (l) m,nh(l) i+m,j+n), where the weights W (l) m,n are shared across spatial locations. This inductive bias enables ecient learning of hierarchical features while preserving spatial relationships (Prince, ). In seismic data analysis, CNNs have demonstrated superior performance across multiple tasks: from denoising and interpolation (Yu and Ma, ) to the identication of geological structures such as faults, horizons, and salt bodies (Mousavi et al., ). The U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., ) addresses a critical limitation in traditional CNNs: the trade-obetween spatial resolution and semantic context. Its symmetric encoder-decoder structure, augmented with skip connections s(l) : h(l) enc →h(l) dec, enables the integration of multi-scale features. This design preserves ne-grained spatial details while incorporating broader contextual information—a capability particularly crucial for semantic segmentation tasks. Originally developed for biomedical image segmentation, the architecture’s eectiveness in maintaining both local precision and global context has led to its widespread adoption across domains, including seismic interpretation. .Reﬂection Seismology Reection seismology forms the foundation of subsurface imaging. The method relies on analyzing elastic or acoustic waves that propagate through the Earth: when these waves encounter interfaces between materials with contrasting acoustic impedances, they partition into transmitted and reected components. The reected waves are recorded by surface sensors as time-series data, with each individual recording termed a seismic Chapter . Mathematically, a seismic trace s(t) can be expressed as the convolution of the source wavelet w(t) with the Earth’s reectivity function r(t), plus noise n(t): s(t) = w(t) ∗r(t) + n(t) (Biloti, ) Data acquisition geometries are optimized according to survey objectives and environmental constraints. Land surveys typically employ linear receiver arrays with multiple source points, while marine surveys utilize vessel-towed hydrophone streamers. The spatial sampling interval between receivers, determines the horizontal resolution of the waveeld reconstruction. Similarly, the temporal sampling rate, typically in milliseconds, must be sucient to capture the highest frequencies of interest. The recorded data is organized into gathers based on common acquisition parameters, such as source location or reection point. However, raw seismic records are contaminated by various noise sources: near-surface eects, ambient noise, and multiple reections all obscure the primary reections of interest (Biloti, ; Yilmaz, ). Modern surveys generate massive datasets as acquisitions routinely produce hundreds of thousands of traces. Thus, the scale and complexity of seismic data, combined with the presence of various noise types, demands for extensive pre-processing and processing steps to obtain accurate subsurface images. .First Break Picking First breaks in seismic data represent the initial arrival times of seismic energy at receiver locations. Their accurate identication is crucial for seismic data pre-processing, particularly in land acquisitions where they enable static corrections—time shifts applied to each trace to compensate for near-surface velocity variations and topographic eects (Marsden, ). First-break times also support various applications including the modeling of water velocity variations (Mardan et al., ) and the renement of velocity models for top salt structures in deep-water reservoirs through travel time tomography (Alves Chapter . Literature Review and Theoretical Foundation et al., ). ..Traditional Methods The increasing scale of seismic surveys has motivated the development of automated rst-break picking methods. Early approaches focused on detecting abrupt changes in signal characteristics. The Short-Term Average/Long-Term Average (STA/LTA) method (Coppens, ) compares energy ratios between short and long time windows to identify sudden amplitude variations. While these methods perform adequately on data with high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), their reliability deteriorates in noisy conditions due to their susceptibility to spurious amplitude spikes. Further developments sought to enhance robustness to noise. The Modied Energy Ratio (MER) method (Wong, ) incorporates preprocessing techniques such as multi- trace summation to improve SNR before applying the picking algorithm. While MER shows improved performance under moderate noise conditions, it remains susceptible to errors when processing weak signals or data with severe noise contamination. An alternative approach based on fractal analysis (Boschetti et al., ) identies rst breaks by detecting variations in fractal dimension along seismic traces, attempting to capture the transition from noise to signal. However, these methods, despite their sophistication in emulating human pattern recognition, impose signicant computational costs while still exhibiting limitations in low SNR scenarios Despite these advancements, a signicant limitation shared by many of these al- gorithms is their reliance on processing traces individually, which overlooks the spatial coherence between adjacent traces and exacerbates inaccuracies under challenging con- ditions. Consequently, automated picking methods often struggle to achieve satisfactory results when dealing with low-quality or noisy data, requiring substantial manual interven- tion to correct errors and rene picks. As a result, a considerable amount of human eort is still necessary to ensure reliable rst-break picking in complex datasets. ..Deep Learning approaches Hence, rst break picking has emerged as a domain where the sophisticated pat- tern recognition capabilities of modern machine learning techniques prove particularly advantageous, as demonstrated by numerous studies evaluating their eectiveness. The problem is commonly formulated as a semantic segmentation task, where each seismogram is processed as an image whose pixels are classied into predened classes. St-Charles et al. () established a benchmark for rst break picking on publicly available seismic data using U-Nets. Their work demonstrated the generalizability of this approach by conducting training, validation, and testing across dierent seismic sites. The Chapter . Literature Review and Theoretical Foundation study included hyperparameter optimization, evaluation of various encoding/decoding paths, and identication of optimal combinations for each fold. Similarly, Zwartjes and Yoo () evaluated dierent U-Net architectural parameters, including network depth, loss functions, and input sizes. Loginov et al. () introduced a D convolutional network workow that achieves comparable results to U-Net while requiring fewer computational resources and only thousands of traces for training. However, the applicability of their model across dierent seismic settings remains uncertain, as D CNNs cannot capture the spatial context that is crucial in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) scenarios, where individual traces may become indistinguishable from noise without considering their neighboring traces. Both Li et al. () and Yuan et al. () propose more sophisticated workows. Li et al. () employs a parallel structure combining a U-Net and a denoising convolutional network to implement a ne-tuning approach that incorporates knowledge of noisy data. Yuan et al. () introduces a dual-model architecture comprising a U-Net for semantic segmentation and a recurrent neural network for selecting rst picks among the inferred classes. The authors develop a straightforward transfer learning scheme where weights from a previously trained network are applied when training on dierent eld data geometries, using only a few newly annotated shots. Their results demonstrate that this approach yields improved evaluation metrics compared to naive weight initialization. Similarly, Mardan et al. () explored transfer learning by ne-tuning pre-trained ImageNet networks with minimal new annotated examples. However, while their results suggested improved performance through pre-training, the validation was limited by both a small test set and ambiguous evaluation metrics. These approaches aim to leverage knowledge gained from networks trained on initial datasets through weight ne-tuning, thus minimizing the need for new manual annotations. This strategy for generalization diers fundamentally from St-Charles et al. ()’s approach of learning generalizable features from one dataset for direct application to others. However, both methodologies require substantial previously annotated gathers for initial training. For such approaches to be eciently employed, their generalization capacity must be robust enough to minimize further intensive data preparation needs. Proper validation of these methods would require testing on large volumes of fully annotated gathers—a challenge given the typical access restrictions on seismic data. Nevertheless, St-Charles et al. () advances this eort by releasing labeled data for four distinct eld datasets. Beyond architectural choices and training strategies, the reliability of automated picking methods heavily depends on their ability to validate their own predictions. Several approaches have incorporated quality control mechanisms to evaluate and rene picks. Chapter . Literature Review and Theoretical Foundation Loginov et al. () implements a straightforward statistical ltering method, grouping picks by oset bins and discarding those outside a three σ window, using the proportion of retained picks as an indicator of model robustness. Taking a more comprehensive approach, Pu et al. () developed a three-step validation process: statistical ltering, trace coherence assessment using Dynamic Time Warping (Müller, ), and cross-validation of predictions across dierent trace representations. These quality control methods provide valuable performance insights beyond traditional metrics, suggesting that robust automated workows should incorporate systematic validation mechanisms. Such validation strategies, along with fundamental design choices like classication schemes and data preprocessing approaches, form the foundation of an eective rst break picking system, aspects we will examine in detail in our methodology Aligning with Loginov et al. (), we argue that rst break picking models should not prioritize high generalizability. Although CNNs exhibit translation and rotation invariance, they lack scale invariance. The model lters learn features specic to their training seismograms’ resolution and therefore struggle to generalize across geometries with varying trace counts per gather and sampling rates. While theoretically possible to learn scale-invariant features by training on diverse datasets with dierent resolutions, this would require more complex architectures capable of learning a broader feature set, substantially increasing computational demands. Moreover, even such enhanced models could fail when encountering signicantly dierent resolutions. This practical limitation is supported by Zwartjes and Yoo (), who found that network performance improves as input sizing approaches original dimensions. Furthermore, seismic images lack the rich, distinctive details that enable convolutional models to capture universal features, as they do with natural images Therefore, we propose that a reliable automated workow for rst break picking should emphasize a precise framework capable of generalizing picks from limited examples, while incorporating robust validation mechanisms to ensure reliability. Such an approach would aim to reduce manual labeling requirements through eective use of limited training data and systematic quality control of predictions. The developments reviewed in this chapter highlight the evolution of rst break picking methods from simple signal processing approaches to sophisticated deep learning solutions. While each advancement has brought valuable insights, signicant challenges remain in balancing computational capability with practical constraints. Traditional methods demonstrated the importance of robust signal detection but struggled with noise and trace independence. Modern deep learning approaches showcase the power of spatial context and pattern recognition but face challenges in generalization and data requirements. These observations have guided the development of our methodology, presented in the following chapter, which aims to address these challenges through a focused approach to Chapter . The framework integrates preprocessing, model architecture, and a self-training workow to address the challenge of limited labeled data in seismic interpretation. This approach combines data normalization techniques from computer vision with domain-specic quality controls for seismic data, enabling progressive expansion of the training dataset through controlled self-labeling. .Framework Overview The proposed iterative training framework consists of several interconnected stages, as illustrated in Figure . The workow begins with dataset and model initialization, followed by the selection of initial training gathers. These gathers are split into training and validation sets to provide the foundation for the rst training cycle. The core of the framework is an iterative loop that progressively expands the labeled dataset. Each iteration consists of three main phases: model training, inference on new gathers, and quality control. After training, the model selects new gathers for inference based on their spatial distribution and signal quality. These predictions then undergo a two-stage quality control process. The quality control process begins with a condence ltering step, where predictions are evaluated based on their softmax probabilities. Subsequently, a tau-oset analysis ensures spatial coherence of the picks within each gather. Gathers that pass both quality control stages have their picks incorporated into the training dataset, while failed gathers are discarded from the current iteration. After each iteration, the framework checks dataset coverage to determine if the desired proportion of total gathers (% in our experiments) has been used in training, validation, or inference. If this threshold has not been reached, the process continues with updated training and validation sets incorporating the newly validated picks. Methodology .Model Architecture The implementation uses a U-Net architecture for seismic data segmentation. The network processes seismic gathers as input, with dimensions of traces × time samples, applying padding to accommodate varying gather sizes. The architecture follows an encoder-decoder structure with skip connections. While various deep learning architectures could potentially be employed for this task, we focus on the U-Net architecture to demonstrate our iterative training framework. This choice is motivated by U-Net’s proven eectiveness in seismic interpretation tasks, as demonstrated by previous studies (St-Charles et al., ; Zwartjes and Yoo, ). Each convolution block in the network consists of two sequential ×convolutions. The convolutions use zero padding with size and stride , maintaining spatial dimensions while expanding the feature space. These convolutions operate without bias terms, relying on the subsequent batch normalization layers to center the feature distributions. Following each convolution, a batch normalization layer standardizes the feature maps, facilitating stable gradient ow during training. The normalized outputs pass through a leaky ReLU activation function with a negative slope of ., allowing for small negative gradients that help prevent dead neurons. The contracting path applies these double convolution blocks sequentially, with each block followed by a ×max pooling operation. This progression reduces spatial dimensions while increasing feature depth through [, , , ] channels. The expanding path mirrors this structure, using transposed convolutions for upsampling. Skip connections transfer feature maps from the contracting path to the upsampled outputs, maintaining spatial precision through the decoding process. The nal layer applies a ×convolution to produce two feature maps matching the input dimensions, representing the background and rst-break classes. .Datasets The experiments use two mining seismic datasets from Canada: Halfmile, and Sudbury, as provided by St-Charles et al. Source: author CONCLUSION This work presented an iterative deep learning framework for rst-break detec- tion that prioritizes adaptability to specic seismic datasets over pursuit of universal generalization. The Halfmile dataset showed signicant improvements through iterative training, reducing mean absolute error from .to .samples while maintaining high window accuracy (Awindow = ▷), while the more modest gains with Sudbury reect the framework’s sensibility to varying data quality. The contrast in performance between datasets validates our fundamental design philosophy: rather than attempting to create a broadly generalizable model, we focused on developing a workow that eciently learns from limited high-quality examples within specic survey contexts. This approach better serves practical requirements in seismic processing, where reliable picks for a particular survey are more valuable than a generalized but potentially less accurate solution. .Limitations While these results demonstrate the potential of our adaptable workow approach, some limitations should be acknowledged that represent opportunities for future research and potential improvements to the methodology. The number of experimental runs (repetitions per conguration) limits the statistical robustness of our results. A larger number of trials would provide more reliable estimates of the model’s performance variability and enable more rigorous statistical analysis. This limitation was primarily due to computational resource and time constraints. Our quality control approach relies on empirically chosen parameters without extensive optimization. The potential biases introduced by the condence thresholding and spatial coherence ltering were not thoroughly investigated. Alternative QC metrics or approaches might provide better selection criteria for the iterative training process. Furthermore, the interaction between QC parameters and nal model performance remains to be systematically analyzed. Some aspects of the training process warrant further investigation. The impact of weight reset intervals on model convergence and stability was not extensively studied. The chosen training/validation split ratios and learning rate policies were based on common practices rather than systematic optimization. Alternative training schedules might provide better convergence or more stable results. Chapter . Conclusion The framework’s sensitivity to preprocessing choices and acquisition parameters was not comprehensively evaluated. While basic trace normalization proved eective, alternative preprocessing approaches might yield better results. The impact of varying acquisition parameters within datasets, such as receiver spacing and sampling rates, on model performance remains unexplored. .Future Work Future research should extend this philosophy of adaptable workows while sys- tematically addressing current limitations. Enhancement of the training process via: • Optimization of weight reset strategies • Investigation of adaptive learning rate schedules • Development of dynamic training/validation split criteria These directions for future research underscore a key insight from our work: the path to better automated rst-break picking may not lie in pursuing universal generalization, but rather in developing workows that can eciently adapt to specic survey requirements. While our framework demonstrates promising results and clear paths for improvement, its primary contribution may be this shift in perspective—from seeking a single, general Chapter .
